NINE YARDS OF ELEGANCE: DRAPED TO TAILORED

How British colonialism altered traditional Indian sarees

Before British influence, Indian women draped sarees in diverse regional styles such as nivi, mundu, madisar, athpourey, etc.
In many traditions, especially in warmer climates, women did not wear blouses or petticoats, the saree itself provided coverage.This was normal, elegant, and modest within Indian cultural norms.
When the British arrived, they viewed local customs through their Victorian moral code, which demanded that women cover their arms, shoulders, and chests.
British officials and missionaries described Indian women’s draping styles as “immodest” or “uncivilized.”
This led to moral policing and the introduction of Western modesty standards, especially in missionary schools and elite households.
This was normal, elegant, and modest within Indian cultural norms.

Harold Dearden’s Indian Women in Trafalgar Square, London

The blouse and petticoat became standard through a mix of colonial pressure and social aspiration.
Elite and educated women — especially in Bengal and Bombay — began to wear Victorian-style bodices under sarees, viewing them as markers of modernity and respectability.
Indian tailors and reformers localized these garments — blouses were redesigned with Indian fabrics and motifs, and petticoats allowed the saree to be worn in new draping styles

ORIGIN OF BATIK

A moment when traditional textile practices influenced emerging modern design movements.

The technique of Batik originated in Indonesia, from the island of Java. The Indonesian people wear Batik to events, both formal and otherwise. It is used in various rituals ceremonies celebrations and daily use as well.Initially, batik making techniques only used batik tulis or written batik techniques. This batik tulis is known as the original because the process and workmanship are still very traditional and manual. Then the technique developed with the discovery of the batik cap or 'stamped batik’ technique which made batik work faster. ’Painted batik’ or batik lukis is a technique of making batik by painting on a white cloth using a medium or a combined medium like canting, brush, banana stalk, broomsticks, cotton or other media.

Indonesian family members producing batik textiles using the traditional wax-resist technique.

During the Art Nouveau period, batik was already a long-established textile technique in Indonesia and parts of South Asia, using wax-resist dyeing to create layered, flowing patterns. As European designers became increasingly interested in non-Western art through colonial trade and exhibitions, batik textiles entered museums and collections, including in India. The organic lines, floral motifs, and repeating patterns of batik closely aligned with Art Nouveau’s focus on nature, craftsmanship, and surface decoration. While batik remained rooted in its cultural traditions, its visual language influenced Art Nouveau textile design and helped shape a broader exchange between Eastern craft practices and Western modern design.

ART DECO'S EFFECT ON INDIAN ARCHITECTURE

How Art Deco has carried on an enduring love affair with India.

Art Deco, characterized by geometric patterns, symmetry, stylized ornamentation, and luxurious materials, embodied the optimism of industrial modernity and machine-age elegance.
As the style spread globally through travel, cinema, and colonial networks, it became a symbol of modern life and cosmopolitan sophistication.
By the early 1930s, Art Deco began arriving in Indian cities such as Bombay (Mumbai) through architects, traders, and the emerging elite exposed to Western design ideals.
This period marked the beginning of India’s dialogue with modernism.

‘Deco By The Bridge’ by Hashim Badani.

The interwar decades saw a boom in urban construction, particularly in Bombay, which became a laboratory for Art Deco architecture.
Buildings such as the Regal Cinema (1933), Eros Cinema (1938), and New India Assurance Building (1936) exemplified a new urban identity sleek, modern, and forward-looking.
Local architects integrated Indian motifs, tropical adaptations, and vernacular materials, giving rise to what historians now term “Indo-Deco.”

FRANZ FERDINAND'S BRAND IDENTITY

How the famous rock band developed a consistent visual style influenced by bauhaus and modernist principles through decades.

During the early 2000s, Franz Ferdinand emerged as part of a post-punk revival that emphasized both sound and visual identity. Formed in Glasgow in 2002, the band deliberately adopted Bauhaus and modernist aesthetics to present themselves as sharp, experimental, and self-aware. This visual strategy reflected the band’s music, which is characterized by rhythmic precision, clean structures, and an emphasis on clarity and repetition rather than excess.

Franz Ferdinand, Band Photograph (Early 2000s)

The Bauhaus movement (1919–1933) and broader modernist design principles promoted functionality, simplicity, and the integration of form and content. These ideas are visible in Franz Ferdinand’s use of bold typography, geometric compositions, limited color palettes, and strong visual hierarchy. Their album covers and promotional graphics recall modernist posters, where text and image function together as a unified system. This approach positioned the band as concept-driven and design-literate, aligning their visual identity with modernist ideals of purpose and coherence.

(left) Alexander Rodchenko, Books! Poster (1924)
(right)Franz Ferdinand, Franz Ferdinand Album Cover (2004)

By adopting Bauhaus and modernist visual strategies, Franz Ferdinand communicated a clear artistic identity that mirrored their musical ethos. The restraint, structure, and confidence of their visual language reinforced the band’s image as forward-thinking and intellectually engaged, while avoiding decorative excess. In recontextualizing modernist design within contemporary music culture, the band demonstrated how Bauhaus principles could express attitude, rhythm, and identity, not just visual style.

DE STIJL

A radical modernist movement, evolving alongside the more expressive American Art Deco movement.

During the 1920s and 1930s, American Art Deco and the De Stijl movement developed alongside one another as responses to rapid industrialization, technological progress, and changing social conditions. De Stijl emerged in the Netherlands around 1917, led by artists and designers such as Piet Mondrian and Gerrit Rietveld, and promoted a radically abstract visual language based on horizontal and vertical lines, primary colors, and pure geometric form. At the same time, American Art Deco took shape in the United States, particularly in urban centers like New York, where it expressed modernity through architecture, furniture, and interiors inspired by skyscrapers, machines, and consumer culture.

De Stijl Furniture and Object Designs by Gerrit Rietveld (c. 1918–1924)

Both movements shared a commitment to modern design and a rejection of historical ornamentation. Geometry, abstraction, and an interest in new materials were central to both De Stijl and American Art Deco. However, their approaches diverged in intent and expression. De Stijl aimed for universality and visual harmony, reducing design to its most essential elements in pursuit of an ideal, almost spiritual order. American Art Deco, by contrast, embraced stylization and decoration, using geometry to create visually striking and often luxurious objects that celebrated speed, progress, and urban life.

PARALLEL MODERNITIES: MINGEI AND STREAMLINING

Examining how Japanese design evolved alongside industrial modernity.

Japanese design history, as outlined in the article, is rooted in a deep respect for craftsmanship, material honesty, and everyday functionality. The Mingei movement of the early 20th century, led by figures such as Yanagi Sōetsu, emphasized the beauty of ordinary, handmade objects used in daily life. Rather than focusing on luxury or individual authorship, Mingei valued simplicity, utility, and anonymity, principles that shaped Japanese design thinking well beyond the movement itself. Over time, these values evolved alongside modernization, influencing architecture, product design, and industrial production in Japan.

Sony, “TPS-L2” Walkman, 1979 © Sony

As Japan industrialized, traditional ideals of simplicity and function began to merge with modern manufacturing. This shift parallels developments seen in the American Streamlining movement of the 1930s, which emerged in response to mass production, technological progress, and changing consumer culture. Like Streamlining in the United States, modern Japanese design embraced efficiency, smooth forms, and practicality. However, while American Streamlining often emphasized speed, aerodynamics, and futuristic optimism, Japanese modern design retained a quieter visual language rooted in restraint, balance, and material sensitivity.

Honda, Civic, 1972 © Honda

The connection between Japanese design and American Streamlining lies in their shared response to modern life through functional, accessible design. Both movements sought to humanize industrial production—Streamlining by making machines and products appear sleek and approachable, and Japanese design by integrating modern forms with long-standing cultural values of simplicity and use. Together, they demonstrate how different cultural contexts shaped parallel design approaches during the 20th century, blending tradition with modernity to redefine everyday objects.

TYPEFACE DESIGN

A moment when typeface design across different scripts responded to shared technological shifts while addressing very different cultural needs.

During the time I Am Martin emerged (around 2016), the typeface design condition in India was undergoing a significant transformation. Indian designers were increasingly engaging with digital type design, open-source distribution, and screen-based readability, particularly for complex scripts like Devanagari. Foundries and collectives such as Ek Type were focused on correcting long-standing gaps in Indic typography by creating fonts that were structurally sound, versatile, and usable across print and digital platforms. The emphasis was less on expressive display and more on functionality, linguistic accuracy, and accessibility for everyday use.

Martin, type specimen.

Ananda Devanagari, developed around this period, reflects these priorities clearly. It is rooted in the traditional structure of the Devanagari script, maintaining clarity in the shirorekha, balanced proportions, and legible matras. Its design responds to the technical challenges of Devanagari—such as complex ligatures and vowel positioning—while adapting them for contemporary digital use. In contrast, I Am Martin is a Latin display typeface that leans heavily into expression, personality, and visual attitude. It is bold, informal, and intentionally imperfect, designed to communicate emotion rather than linguistic precision.

Ananda Devnagri, May 2016.

The similarity between Ananda Devanagari and I Am Martin lies in their shared contemporary context: both are products of a time when designers were reclaiming authorship through digital tools and independent type design. However, their differences highlight how cultural and script-based needs shape typographic outcomes. While I Am Martin prioritizes individuality and visual impact, Ananda Devanagari prioritizes continuity, usability, and cultural responsibility. Together, they illustrate how mid-2010s typography evolved globally—one through expressive freedom, the other through careful modernization of tradition.

Ananda Devnagri, May 2016.

CONTEMPORARY INDIAN ART

Emerging in the early 1990s, contemporary Indian art is characterised by a rise in postmodernist practices based in performance, video, installation and digital media, in addition to traditional disciplines such as painting and sculpture. Experiments with these new mediums are often the result of cross-pollination and collaborations across varied artistic fields such as fashion, film, animation and theatre. The movement originated with India’s economic liberalisation of 1991, and saw a boom period in the early 2000s.

Lady with an Ermine; Bharti Kher; 2012; Mixed media; 230 x 60 x 60 cm; Kiran Nadar Museum of Art, New Delhi

Postmodernist ideas began to develop in Indian art between the 1960s and 1980s through the work of artists like Navjot Altaf, Nalini Malani and Nilima Sheikh, who were trained in conventional mediums but whose practices evolved to include elements that are regarded as distinctly contemporary, namely, performance, installation and digital media. The careers and interests of many contemporary Indian art practitioners that began in the 1990s and 2000s have been shaped by the increased presence of private institutions, rapid technological shifts of the information age and the demands of a globalised art market.
